Limeade - Experimental Glitch Game

Limeade is an experimental, glitch art inspired game, that utilizes pastel colours to create a strange but weirdly wonderful digital world.

itch.io Download

Limeade emerged out of the desire to combine a glitch art aesthetic with a pastel palette to create something new and cute. Taking inspiration from other non-AAA isometric titles, such as Bastion and Transistor, Limeade aims to make use of the isometric style in harmony with the setup of the models and exploration. The player themselves is represented as a citrus representation of an antivirus symbol – the strong shape resembling that of a shield, while taking on a quirky personified form.

Limeade is broken into two distinct sections:

  1. A ‘real world’ with a slightly more realistic room setting, which served both as a mellow introduction to the control scheme, and to build the menu directly into the world-space
  2. A less realistic, computerized glitch world that the player is intended to explore, both to gain small collectibles and to ‘fix’ the computer in the real world. Here, personifications of the computer also live, such as starting off on the ‘home’ screen (represented with a home) and progressing further into chaos, while avoiding the ‘viruses’ within.

You can check out some of the models for this project here on my sketchfab page!

Future direction for this project would be completing more modular 3d models that could be reused more frequently throughout the game. As well, I would’ve liked to have done more animation, now that I know how to do it. Animating the main character, and one of the walls, outside of Unity, and animating the cameras and some additional movement inside of Unity was a wonderful challenge and something I will continue to practice. Then, more models can be made less static in the game itself to create a more vibrant world, with more than one level! Being able to expand into more complicated stages would be an entirely different project with a much larger team, or over a longer period of time. For this project, I feel it was successful with a small team of three, where we were all learning something new.
